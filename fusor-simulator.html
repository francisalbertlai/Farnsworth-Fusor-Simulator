Output

Neutron rate iThe fusor's product. D–D fusion spits out 2.45 MeV neutrons; being neutral, they ignore all fields and fly straight out through the chamber wall. They're what you'd actually measure to prove fusion is happening — a good amateur fusor does 10⁵–10⁷ n/s.: – n/s

Fusion reactions iAll fusion events per second. In D–D, only about half produce a neutron — the other branch makes tritium + a proton. (Amateur fusors literally breed trace tritium; the amounts are utterly negligible.)–
Fusion probability per ion iThe core problem, in one number. The chance a single ion fuses before it dies on the grid — typically 1 in a million or worse. Every ion that doesn't fuse still cost you full acceleration energy. This ratio is fundamentally why fusors can't break even.–
Cross-section at ion energy iHow big a target each nucleus is, measured in barns (10⁻²⁸ m² — physicist humor: "big as a barn"). It grows steeply with energy because faster ions tunnel through the electric repulsion between nuclei more easily. Compare it on the chart below.–
Input power iV × I — what the power supply burns. Nearly all of it ends up as heat in the grid and X-rays from the chamber, not fusion.–
Fusion power iEnergy released by fusion per second. Reactions/s × energy per reaction (3.65 MeV avg for D–D, 17.6 MeV for D–T). Compare it to input power and weep.–
Q (fusion / input power) iThe number all of fusion chases. Q > 1 means more fusion energy out than electrical energy in. ITER targets Q = 10; NIF briefly hit Q ≈ 1.5 (by its own accounting); fusors live around 10⁻⁸. The gains and losses scale with the same knobs — that's the trap.–

Assumptions (v0 — honest list)

Idealized beam-target model. All ions assumed to reach full grid voltage; real fusors lose most ion energy to charge exchange — real rates run ~5–50× below ideal, so a flat ×0.2 degradation factor is applied. No space charge, no beam–beam fusion, ideal spherical symmetry, monoenergetic ions. Particle motion on screen is illustrative (not to physical timescale); the numbers panel is the actual model. Cross-sections: Duane coefficients, NRL Plasma Formulary. D–T shown for physics comparison only — tritium is not obtainable for amateur devices, and no fusor of any design reaches net energy.
